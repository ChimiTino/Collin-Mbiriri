The Challenge

A sign that told
the wrong story

Walk into any shopping mall in South Africa and look for a baby changing station. You'll find it — inside the women's bathroom. The sign on the door: a woman changing a baby. This wasn't just a logistical oversight. It was a symbol of something bigger.

A large percentage of South African fathers still believe that caring for children is a woman's responsibility. As South Africa's largest baby retailer, PEP had both the platform and the obligation to challenge that narrative — but it needed to do so with action, not just words.

The Invisible Father

Baby changing stations in South Africa were almost exclusively located inside women's bathrooms — making fathers feel unwelcome in one of parenting's most basic moments.

A Cultural Gap

Many South African men still default to the belief that child-rearing is a mother's domain — a mindset entrenched across generations and reinforced by everyday infrastructure.

PEP's Position

As the country's biggest baby store, PEP had the reach, the trust, and the responsibility to lead a national conversation about modern fatherhood.

Campaign Film

The Insight

Parenting should be
everyone's role

The insight was deceptively simple: if you want fathers to show up for parenting, you need to design spaces that invite them in. Infrastructure shapes behaviour. When the only changing station in a mall is in the women's bathroom, it sends a clear — if unspoken — message: this isn't for you.

"We believe that parenting should be everyone's role. So we decided to change the parenting conversation in South Africa, starting in one very important area — baby changing stations."

The Father's Day timing wasn't arbitrary. It was the perfect cultural moment to elevate the conversation — when South Africa was already thinking about what fatherhood means and what it should look like.

The Idea

Build the space.
Change the story.

On Father's Day, PEP introduced PEP Changing Stations — purpose-built, dad-friendly baby changing stations installed in accessible areas of shopping malls across South Africa. Not tucked away in a women's bathroom. Not an afterthought. Front and centre, in spaces fathers could actually use.

In partnership with Growthpoint — one of South Africa's largest property developers — PEP created safe, welcoming spaces designed with fathers in mind. The station walls featured warm, encouraging design with mini tutorials, making even first-time fathers feel equipped rather than excluded.

The idea was physical, but the impact was cultural: a changing station isn't just a piece of furniture. It's a declaration about who parenting belongs to.

The Execution

From a station
to a movement

The campaign was fully integrated — physical spaces were just the beginning. The conversation was carried across every platform that could reach South African parents.

The Results

A station changed.
A conversation started.

PEP Changing Stations did what great purpose-driven work should: it didn't just talk about change — it built it. By creating physical spaces for fathers in malls across South Africa, PEP shifted a cultural norm in the most literal way possible.

The campaign generated widespread organic conversation on social media, with parents across the country sharing their perspectives on what modern parenting should look like. On air and online, the dialogue extended well beyond Father's Day — embedding the idea that parenting is everyone's role into South Africa's cultural conversation.

For PEP, the campaign reinforced its position not just as South Africa's largest baby retailer, but as a brand that genuinely champions the families it serves — fathers included.

"When you change the infrastructure, you change the conversation. When you change the conversation, you start to change the culture."
